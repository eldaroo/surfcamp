Santa Teresa is no longer a secret — but it has managed to stay genuinely special in a way that many once-hidden surf destinations haven't. Located on the southern tip of Costa Rica's Nicoya Peninsula, this stretch of Pacific coastline offers the rare combination of world-class surf, a thriving wellness scene, stunning natural beauty, and a community that still feels more village than resort.

Whether you are planning your first visit or returning for another season, this travel guide covers everything you need to know about Santa Teresa in 2026.

## Where Is Santa Teresa?

Santa Teresa sits on the Nicoya Peninsula in Puntarenas province, on Costa Rica's Pacific coast. The nearest towns are Cobano (15 km inland, where the bank and larger supermarkets are) and Mal País (10 km south, quieter and more rugged). The main stretch of Santa Teresa runs from Playa Carmen in the south through Carmen, Santa Teresa proper, and up to Playa Hermosa in the north — about 8 km of beach road in total.

## When to Go

Costa Rica's Pacific coast has two distinct seasons, and both have genuine appeal for visitors to Santa Teresa.

### Dry Season (December – April)
- Consistent offshore winds in the morning create clean, glassy surf
- Low rainfall, warm days, cooler nights
- Busiest and most expensive time to visit
- Best for first-time visitors who want reliable good weather

### Green Season (May – November)
- Afternoon tropical showers (usually clearing by evening)
- Lush, vivid landscapes — the jungle is at its most alive
- Fewer tourists, lower prices on accommodation and activities
- September and October bring the most powerful surf swells
- Best for experienced travelers, budget-conscious visitors, and surfers chasing waves

**Our recommendation:** May and June offer an excellent balance — the landscape is beautiful, the surf is consistent, crowds have thinned, and prices drop noticeably.

## How to Get to Santa Teresa

Getting to Santa Teresa requires a little planning, but the journey is genuinely part of the experience.

### Option 1: San Jose → Ferry → Santa Teresa (4–5 hours)
The most popular route for international arrivals into San Jose (SJO):
1. Bus or shuttle from San Jose to Puntarenas (2 hours)
2. Ferry across the Gulf of Nicoya to Paquera (1.5 hours, runs every 2 hours)
3. Bus or shared shuttle from Paquera to Santa Teresa (1.5 hours)

Shuttle services like Interbus or Caribe Shuttle handle the entire route as a single booking, which simplifies logistics considerably.

### Option 2: Domestic Flight to Tambor
Several airlines fly from San Jose's Tobias Bolanos airport (SYQ) to Tambor (TMU), about 45 minutes by air. From Tambor, it is a 45-minute drive to Santa Teresa. More expensive but dramatically faster.

### Option 3: Liberia (LIR) → Santa Teresa
If you are flying into Liberia — common for North Americans on direct flights — the drive takes about 3.5–4 hours via the Nicoya Peninsula road network. Car rental from Liberia gives you flexibility; the roads require a 4WD during green season.

## Getting Around

Santa Teresa's main road (unpaved in sections) runs the length of the beach. Getting around is easy:

- **Motorbike or ATV rental**: The most popular option, allows flexibility and gets you to surf spots quickly. Expect $35–50/day.
- **Bicycle**: Fine for the flat central section, challenging on hilly stretches.
- **Taxi**: Available and reasonably priced for short trips. Ask your accommodation to call one.
- **On foot**: The central area around Playa Carmen is very walkable.

## Where to Stay

Santa Teresa offers accommodation across all price ranges:

- **Surf camps and hostels**: Shared rooms from $25–40/night, often including breakfast and activity access
- **Boutique guesthouses**: Private rooms from $60–100/night
- **Private cabins and villas**: From $150/night for standalone units with full amenities
- **Luxury boutique hotels**: Several high-end properties at $200+/night

The most important factor is location relative to the beach. Being within walking distance of the water means you can respond to good conditions at any time of day.

## What to Eat

Santa Teresa's food scene is better than its size would suggest:

- **Sodas**: Traditional Costa Rican restaurants serving rice, beans, salad, and fresh fish for $5–8. Soda Playa Carmen and similar local spots are excellent value.
- **International restaurants**: Thai, Italian, Japanese, and Mediterranean options are all available along the main road.
- **Health food**: Smoothie bowls, acai, raw food, and vegan menus are easy to find — the wellness culture extends to eating.
- **Groceries**: There is a small supermarket in town. Larger shopping requires a trip to Cobano.

**Must try:** Fresh ceviche, whole grilled fish, and the green season mangos that fall from roadside trees in July and August.

## Practical Information

### Money
Santa Teresa is increasingly card-friendly, but carry colones (the local currency) for small purchases, local sodas, and tips. The nearest ATM is in Cobano — withdraw what you need.

### Internet and Phone
Most accommodation and cafés have WiFi. Mobile coverage is decent on main roads (Claro and Kolbi have the best coverage in the peninsula).

### Safety
Santa Teresa is generally very safe for tourists. Standard precautions apply: don't leave valuables in cars, use a locker at the beach, and swim between the flags if you are not a confident swimmer.

### Surf Safety
Ocean currents can be strong at certain breaks. Respect rip currents, never surf alone in remote areas, and be honest with instructors about your level. The sea here is beautiful and powerful in equal measure.

## How Many Days Do You Need?

- **3 days**: Enough for a taste — a couple of surf sessions, a yoga class, a sunset
- **1 week**: The sweet spot for most visitors. Enough time to see improvement in the water and feel the rhythm of the place
- **2+ weeks**: For those who want to go deep — real surf progression, a genuine community experience, transformation that sticks

Santa Teresa rewards longer stays. The town reveals itself slowly, and the best experiences — the early morning lineup with no one out, the impromptu beach dinner with new friends, the day you finally pop up clean — don't happen on a schedule.

Come open, stay curious, and the Nicoya Peninsula will deliver.
